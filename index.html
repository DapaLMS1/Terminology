The Building Blocks of Medical Language

Medical terms are built from three main elements: a Prefix, a Root Word, and a Suffixs. Understanding these parts is the key to quickly defining any complex medical term.

1. Prefix (The Beginning)

It modifies the root word to indicate location (like Sub- for below), time (like Pre- for before), intensity (like Hyper- for excessive), or number. Not all medical terms have a prefix.

2. Root Word (The Foundation)

This part provides the fundamental meaning and typically refers to a specific body part (like Gingiv- for gums), structure, or bodily system (like Cardi- for heart). It is mandatory in almost all complex medical terms.

3. Suffix (The Ending)

This element determines what the entire term is describing, usually a procedure (like -ectomy for removal), a condition (like -itis for inflammation), or a disease (like -osis for abnormal state).

Prefix Components

Brady-

Slow

For example, Bradycardia is a condition defined by a slow heart rate.
Tachy-

Fast

For example, Tachypnoea is the medical term for rapid breathing.
Hemi-

Half

For example, Hemiplegia is paralysis affecting one vertical half of the body.
Poly-

Many / much

For example, Polyuria means the excessive or abnormally large production of urine.
Hypo-

Under

For example, Hypothermia is a term for below normal body temperature.
Pre-

Before

For example, Preoperative means occurring before surgery.
Peri-

Around

For example, Pericoronitis describes inflammation around a tooth crown.
Sub-

Below

For example, Subgingival refers to the location beneath the gumline.
Intra-

Within

For example, Intraoral refers to procedures or areas within the mouth.
Hyper-

Excessive

For example, Hyperplasia is an overgrowth of tissue.
Hypo-

Deficient

For example, Hypocalcification means insufficient calcium in enamel.
Dys-

Abnormal

For example, Dysphagia is the medical term for difficulty swallowing.
Para-

Beside

For example, Parotid describes the salivary gland region near the ear.

Root Word Components

Dent- / Odont-

Tooth

For example, Dentition refers to the arrangement of teeth.
Gingiv-

Gums

For example, Gingivitis is inflammation of the gums.
Enamel / Amel-

Enamel

For example, Hypo-amelogenesis is the under-development of enamel.
Pulp-

Dental pulp

For example, Pulpitis is inflammation of the dental pulp.
Alveol-

Socket

For example, The Alveolar ridge is the bone supporting teeth.
Or- / Stomat-

Mouth

For example, Stomatitis is inflammation of the mouth.
Maxill- / Mandibul-

Jaw

For example, Maxillary arch refers to the upper jaw structure.
Cephal- / Cran-

Head / skull

For example, Cephalometric analysis is head measurement for orthodontics.
Cardi-

Heart

For example, Cardiology is the study of the heart.

Suffix Components

-algia

Pain

For example, Neuralgia is pain felt along a nerve path.
-ectomy

Surgical removal

For example, Appendectomy is the surgical removal of the appendix.
-oma

Tumour / growth

For example, Carcinoma is a malignant tumor arising from epithelial cells.
-pathy

Disease

For example, Neuropathy is a general term for a disease or dysfunction of nerves.
-itis

Inflammation

For example, Gingivitis is inflammation of the gums.
-ectomy

Surgical removal (Dental Context)

For example, Odontectomy means surgical removal of a tooth.
-dynia

Pain

For example, Pleurodynia is sharp pain in the side due to muscle spasms.
-osis

Condition

For example, Halitosis is the condition commonly known as bad breath.
-plasty

Surgical repair

For example, Gingivoplasty is the surgical repair or reshaping of gum tissue.
-logy / -logist

Study

For example, A Periodontologist is a specialist in gum diseases.
-gram / -graph

Record

For example, A Radiograph is an image produced by X-rays.
